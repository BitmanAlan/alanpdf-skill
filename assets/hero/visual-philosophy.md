# Institutional Signal

This visual direction treats business documents as artifacts of decision-making rather than office exports. The page should feel precise, measured, and expensive without becoming loud. Space carries authority. Surfaces stay bright and disciplined. Color appears in deliberate signals rather than floods, as if every accent exists to mark confidence, hierarchy, and intent.

The composition should suggest a product that sits between consulting craft and capital-markets polish. Large quiet fields create trust. A few anchored shapes, thin rules, and restrained gradients introduce energy without spectacle. The visual center of gravity comes from layered document planes, implying that the system can express different document genres while preserving one coherent standard of quality.

Typography should feel exacting and calm. Words are not decoration and never dominate the canvas. The headline is a control surface: clear, strong, and intelligently understated. Supporting labels behave like metadata chips from premium working documents. Nothing should look playful, inflated, or trend-driven. Every alignment should feel measured by a designer obsessed with margins, rhythm, and proportion.

Color should encode roles rather than moods. Deep consulting blue establishes trust and structure. Emerald introduces executive polish. A restrained warm red can appear as a small analytical counterweight, echoing pricing warnings or research risk markers. These tones must be used sparingly, against luminous neutrals, so the image feels engineered rather than illustrated.

The final banner should look painstakingly refined. It should suggest a product built by someone who has spent years looking at proposals, pricing memos, and sell-side reports and deciding exactly where generic tools fail. The craftsmanship must be visible in spacing, shadow softness, card overlap, border weight, and the stillness of the overall composition. The goal is not merely a header image. The goal is a quiet proof of taste.
